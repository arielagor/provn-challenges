Meridian's CFO and CIO together. Here is the picture.

You are migrating from on-premise Primous to Oracle EDM Cloud in twelve weeks, with no buffer past week twelve. Fiscal year-end close opens at week fourteen. The Chart of Accounts and Entity hierarchies are the metadata source of truth for FCCS, which means any error in the migration shows up in your consolidated financials. Two business teams use this system every day, a third consumes it, and your governance workflow has to survive the move.

My recommended approach. Two separate EDM Cloud applications, Planning template for the Chart of Accounts because it feeds both FCCS and PBCS, and FCCS template for the Entity hierarchy because the FCCS application template ships with FCCS-specific validations baked in. Phased cutover by hierarchy, not big bang. Migrate the live version plus the trailing four quarters, archive the rest to immutable cold storage with a tested retrieval runbook.

Configuration walkthrough. The biggest design decision is using a single node type for the Chart of Accounts with the eight segments expressed as required properties on the same node, instead of eight separate node types. The reason is simple. FCCS expects a single Account dimension when it consumes metadata. Eight node types means eight validation rule sets, eight workflow definitions, and eight times the chance for hierarchy drift between segments. One node type with property-based segmentation centralizes the governance and matches what the downstream consumer expects.

Validation rules I am putting on that single node type. Account Type to Balance Type consistency, asset and expense are debit, liability and equity and revenue are credit. Hard fail on commit. Leaf only posting, only leaf nodes can have a balance type. FCCS member name conformance, the FCCS-side member name must conform to FCCS character rules. Intercompany pair completeness, if a node is flagged intercompany its pair must exist and be active. Cross-viewpoint consistency, a node's account type cannot differ between the statutory and management viewpoints. These are configured as Cloud-native validations, not custom scripts, which is a meaningful operational improvement over Primous because they run at request time, not commit time.

Two viewpoints over the Chart of Accounts node base. Statutory view feeds FCCS for consolidation. Management view feeds PBCS for budgeting. Both share the same underlying nodes, so when a node is added once it appears in both views with viewpoint-specific parent assignments. This is the cleanest improvement over Primous, where alternate hierarchies were maintained as parallel structures and tended to drift.

The trade-off I made. I am scoping the validation rules to the regulatory and FCCS-impact set on day one, and grandfathering legacy data quality issues with a temporary exception property. The reason is that the validation pre-pass in week eight is going to surface a long tail of legacy issues, and trying to clean all of them in four weeks would either blow the timeline or force the data governance team into a triage spiral. The post-go-live phase two hardening is the right place to tighten cosmetic validations.

Migration and risk. Twelve-week window, three phases. Weeks one to four, analysis and design with a hard sign-off gate at week four. Weeks five to eight, build and load with reconciliation checkpoints. Weeks nine to twelve, UAT and a parallel close validation in week ten on a closed prior period to confirm the EDM-Cloud-fed FCCS produces the same results as Primous-fed FCCS to the penny.

The single biggest risk to this migration is FCCS metadata mismatch at cutover blocking the year-end close. If the EDM Cloud configuration diverges from what FCCS expects, the close opens with a broken metadata source. How I mitigate it. Three layers. The reconciliation checkpoint at week eight runs a full FCCS test sync on a frozen-as-of-week-seven dataset and compares the trial balance to Primous-fed actuals. The week thirteen soak period after both cutovers gives a final detection window before close opens. And Primous remains read-only available through week thirteen as a rollback option, since the on-premise license overlap covers it.

The AI moment. The first cutover plan I asked Claude to draft was big bang on cutover Saturday of week twelve. I rejected it. Big bang in a fiscal year-end window is the riskiest possible choice, because if something breaks on cutover Saturday, the close opens two weeks later with a broken metadata source. I rewrote it as a phased cutover by hierarchy. Entity goes first in week eleven with a four-day soak period, then Chart of Accounts in week twelve, then a full week thirteen soak before close opens. The cost is one extra weekend of work. The value is two real detection-and-rollback windows.

The pattern I am watching for. AI drafts optimize for elegant single-event cutover because it reads better in a writeup. Real migrations optimize for detection windows and rollback options, which usually means more cut points, not fewer.

Reflection. With more time, I would push for a deeper read of Meridian's existing custom Primous integration. The discovery interview will surface the documented integrations, but eight years of on-premise tends to accumulate undocumented hooks, and those are the ones that break at cutover. I would also want to talk to the FCCS owner specifically about which prior-period restatements have happened in the last two years, because that pattern tells me which historical snapshots actually need to be live in EDM Cloud versus which can go to cold storage. And I would want a sit-down with each of the three business teams' actual change-makers, not their managers, because the people clicking the buttons on Friday afternoon know things that nobody else does.
