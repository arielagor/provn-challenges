# Video walkthrough script

Trial-to-paid dropped from 18% to 11% over two quarters. Three suspects: activation, email, Pipestack at $79. I am picking activation on the math. Trial users who reach the Pipeline Health dashboard in 72 hours convert at three times the rate of those who don't. Only 24% get there. Biggest lever on the sheet, nothing close. The decision I am most confident in is fix activation before touching email or price.

Key choices inside the brief. One experiment, not five. First-session guided setup that routes every new trial directly into Pipeline Health. Primary metric: 72-hour activation rate, target 35%. Guardrail: CRM-sync error rate, because pushing users to Pipeline Health before their CRM is clean spikes support tickets and poisons the data. Success threshold: four-point conversion lift at 95% confidence over four weeks.

Considered a re-engagement email sprint to lift the 4% click-through. Rejected. Email drives traffic into a funnel that loses 76% of users in 72 hours. Fixing the channel before the product moment spreads the leak. Considered cutting price to match Pipestack. Rejected. A price cut locks in lower revenue at the exact moment Coach justifies the current price. Coach is the pricing answer, not a discount.

ICP is the second-line sales manager at a 100 to 400 person company, five to fifteen reps under them, coaching from memory on Friday nights. Positioning: Coach is the pipeline coach that sits inside the deal, not next to it. Pipestack shows the pipeline. Coach acts on it.

On GTM. Six weeks, three named dependencies. Product analytics instruments the 72-hour funnel by end of Week 1 or the experiment is blind. Engineering finishes Coach rate-limiting and audit logging by Week 3 or the Week 4 beta is a data incident. VP Sales protects three 90-minute enablement sessions in Weeks 2, 4, and 6. If VP Sales lets reps skip sessions to chase pipeline, those reps are pitching "pipeline visibility" the day Coach ships.

If Pipestack announces AI in Week 3, plan does not blow up. Specific changes. Sales teases Coach in active evals in Week 3 instead of Week 5. Reps get a two-paragraph leave-behind in 48 hours. Week 4 enablement flips to a side-by-side Coach versus Pipestack AI demo. Launch message reframes around Coach acting on Pipeline Health, a phrasing change, not a rewrite, because positioning was built that way from the start.

On AI. The redirection moment. I asked Claude to pick one of three conversion problems and defend it. First pass chose Pipestack because it read more urgent. I pushed back on the math. A 3x lift on 24% adoption is a mechanical lever. Six head-to-head losses on price is narrative pressure. I told Claude to rebuild around activation and keep the other two as explicitly deprioritized. That is the draft that shipped. The lesson: the AI optimizes for the loudest problem if I let it. My job is to keep it honest on the numbers.

Reflection. Another week and twice the budget. I would stand up a lightweight onboarding concierge for the top 200 trial accounts in Week 1. A real person messaging them inside the product to walk them to Pipeline Health. Expensive and does not scale. I do not care. It gives a clean read on whether activation is a product problem or a behavior problem, and it ships Coach into a funnel that has already been hand-proven.

Activation first. Coach as the layer that makes activation matter. Everything else waits.
