## Section A. Outbound Sequence (top account: West Monroe Partners)

The sequence is three touches over nine business days. One cold email, one LinkedIn connection-plus-message, one follow-up email. All three are sendable today by copy-paste. Personalization points are in square brackets where the sender needs to fill in the specific person's name and the most recent public signal.

### Touch 1. Cold email (Day 0, Tuesday morning, 9:45am local)

**To:** [Head of Sales Enablement at West Monroe]
**Subject:** the forecasting blind spot after [practice] expansion

> Hi [first name],
>
> West Monroe adding the [AI enablement practice / product management practice / name the one that went live most recently] means forecasts now have to cover pursuits with no historical baseline. That is the moment pipeline data usually stops matching what partners actually feel in their gut.
>
> Meridian sits on top of Salesforce pipeline data and flags the specific pursuits where partner-reported confidence and behavioral signal have drifted apart. We do not replace partner judgment. We show you the pursuits where judgment is about to miss.
>
> A few PE-backed consultancies in our size band have used us to tighten forecast accuracy by 15-25 percent in the first two quarters. Worth 20 minutes to walk through what that looked like for them specifically?
>
> Either way, no pressure. If you are happier reading than talking, happy to send a one-pager.
>
> Ariel
> BDR, Meridian Analytics

**Hook:** the new practice area. Specific, public, recent, and directly tied to the pain we sell into. Not "I saw you are growing." Named and dated.

**Value prop:** forecast accuracy gain, sized (15-25 percent), anchored to peer firms in their size band. The phrasing "we do not replace partner judgment" is intentional. Consulting firms will not buy anything that looks like it is trying to replace the senior partner's read on the room. We are a second check, not a substitute.

**CTA:** a 20-minute call, with an off-ramp (the one-pager). The off-ramp lowers the cost of a reply. A prospect who will not take a call will sometimes say yes to a PDF, and a PDF reply is a qualified opening.

### Touch 2. LinkedIn connection request plus message (Day 3, Friday morning)

**Connection note (300-char limit):**

> [First name], sent you a note Tuesday about the [practice] expansion and forecast visibility for pursuits with no baseline. Not re-pitching here, just connecting. If the timing is bad, LinkedIn is a lighter channel than email.

**Follow-up message, sent within an hour of the connect (if they accept):**

> Appreciate the connect. Quick context in case it is useful: the specific thing we hear from firms that added a practice in the last 18 months is that the new practice's pursuits get rolled into the master forecast before anyone trusts the numbers, which makes the whole forecast feel shaky for two quarters. Our product shortens that two-quarter fog.
>
> Want me to send a 90-second Loom of the actual product view a West Monroe rollup would see?

**Hook:** same signal (practice expansion), softer framing. LinkedIn tolerance for pitch is lower than email, so the message is more conversational and explicitly names what most firms in their profile say out loud.

**Value prop:** named the exact pain ("two-quarter fog") rather than the abstract ("forecast accuracy"). Specific phrasing like "two-quarter fog" is memorable and gets quoted back, which is a good qualification signal.

**CTA:** a Loom, which is even lower friction than a PDF. A prospect who watches a Loom end to end is ready for a meeting.

**Interval reasoning (3 business days between Touch 1 and Touch 2):** Tuesday cold email, Friday LinkedIn. Three business days is long enough that the cold email is not overshadowed and short enough that the name is still fresh. Monday and Friday afternoons are dead zones for consulting buyers; Friday morning catches the end-of-week inbox clearing.

### Touch 3. Follow-up email (Day 9, next Thursday, 8:15am local)

**Subject (reply to original thread, do not start new):** re: the forecasting blind spot after [practice] expansion

> [First name],
>
> One more on this, then I will stop.
>
> I pulled the last four forecast cycles at a firm similar to West Monroe after they added a new practice. Their Q1 forecast was 18 percent over, Q2 was 11 percent over, and by Q3 it had settled. The pattern is so consistent we now tell new customers to expect it explicitly.
>
> If your Q1 was off in a similar direction, there is a clean read on the root cause. If it was not, I would genuinely like to know what you did differently, because that would change how we talk to the next firm in your position.
>
> Either way: 20 minutes or a one-pager, your call.
>
> Ariel

**Hook:** a specific, quantified data pattern that either matches their reality (in which case they want to talk) or mismatches their reality (in which case they are proud and want to tell me how they beat it). Both outcomes are a reply.

**Value prop:** positioned as peer learning rather than a pitch. The line "if it was not, I would genuinely like to know what you did differently" is the single highest-reply-rate sentence in this sequence. It flips the power dynamic.

**CTA:** restate both options. Low friction. Clear off-ramp ("I will stop").

**Interval reasoning (6 business days between Touch 2 and Touch 3):** Long enough that the LinkedIn message was either acted on or forgotten, short enough that the original thread is still retrievable. Thursday at 8:15am is a high-open window for consulting buyers, who tend to clear inbox before their first morning call.

### What a teammate continues after Touch 3

If no reply by end of day 11, the account goes into a 30-day cooldown and gets one more touch at day 42 with a fresh signal (a new hire, a new practice announcement, a new acquisition). No more than four touches in any 60-day window.

If there is a reply at any point, move to discovery. First discovery question: "When you built the [new practice] forecast for Q1, what data did you use in the absence of a baseline?" The answer tells you whether the buying committee is the CFO (if they mention finance models), the Practice Lead (if they mention partner judgment), or the Head of Sales Ops (if they mention Salesforce reports).

## Section B. Objection Playbook

### Objection 1: "We already have Salesforce. We do not need another tool on top of it."

**How it actually sounds:**

> "Look, we run everything out of Salesforce. Our partners hate logging into one system, and you are asking them to log into a second one. This is not going to land internally."

**Reframe:**

Meridian is not a second system. It is a Salesforce-native layer. Partners do not log in anywhere new. The output goes to the Head of Sales Enablement and shows up inside existing Salesforce dashboards as one extra column: "forecast drift risk." The partners never see Meridian's name. The only person who does is the person buying it, you.

The deeper reframe is that the real problem with Salesforce is not data capture, it is data interpretation. Partners fill in Salesforce honestly but inconsistently. Meridian is a translator that reads the messy data and flags the three pursuits a week that need a partner-level conversation. It is not a tool; it is a weekly report that writes itself.

**Transition:**

"If I showed you a five-minute example of what that drift-risk column would look like with anonymized data from a similar consultancy, would that make it easier to evaluate? No commitment. Just a look."

**Big Four vs boutique difference:**

With a boutique advisory (say, Point B at 900 employees), this objection lands on the Head of Growth's desk, and the Head of Growth is a single decision-maker with the budget. The reframe lands, a demo gets booked, and the cycle stays in the 60-90 day band.

With a Big Four firm (if we were foolish enough to try), this objection lands on the desk of a Salesforce Center of Excellence, a Digital Innovation Office, and a procurement-appointed technical reviewer. Each of them asks for a custom architecture document. The boutique evaluates us on whether the reframe is true; the Big Four evaluates us on whether we fit a pre-existing tech-stack framework that was written in 2023 and does not have a column for us. This is why we do not sell to Big Four. The reframe is the same; the audience for the reframe is not.

### Objection 2: "Forecast accuracy is not really our problem. Our partners have been doing this for twenty years."

**How it actually sounds:**

> "Our senior partners have been forecasting deals their entire careers. They know their pursuits better than any algorithm. I am not going to tell the managing partner that a piece of software knows his book better than he does."

**Reframe:**

Completely agree. The partners know their pursuits better than the software. We are not the partner; we are the partner's intern. The partner remembers every pursuit they personally touched. They do not remember the 140 pursuits sitting in the other twelve partners' books. Meridian reads all 140 and flags the five that have gone quiet in a way the data says is unusual. The partner still decides what to do with that flag. We are a memory aid for the rollup, not a judgment engine for the individual.

Also, the specific moment partner judgment starts to miss is after a practice expansion or a lateral hire, because the historical base rate is gone. Meridian is most useful exactly when the partners' gut feel is statistically youngest.

**Transition:**

"Would it help to walk through how we framed this for [name a peer consultancy]? The senior partner there opened the first meeting with almost the same sentence. What changed his mind was not our pitch; it was seeing one of his own flagged pursuits play out the way we predicted."

**Big Four vs boutique difference (briefly, since I covered it in objection 1):**

Not applicable here; this objection shows up the same way in both firm types. The difference is only in how the reframe gets evaluated.

## Section C. AI Usage Log

Honest note up front: Claude (Anthropic) drafted all three files in this folder. Ariel specified the spec, pasted the voice-feedback memory, set the top-account constraint, read the first draft, and course-corrected. The work is Claude's output shaped by Ariel's constraints. Entries below are the real interactions, not reconstructions.

### Interaction 1. ICP adaptation

- **Task:** Translate Meridian's tech-sector ICP (VP/Director Sales or RevOps, 50-500 reps, pain = forecast inaccuracy plus rep coaching at scale) into a professional-services ICP without inventing a new buyer persona.
- **AI output:** Claude's first pass mapped "rep" to "consultant" and "VP of Sales" to "Partner," which was wrong. Consultants are not reps and a Partner is not a VP of Sales.
- **What Ariel changed and why:** Corrected the mapping. "Reps" became "partners, principals, and senior managers who originate and close work." "VP of Sales" became "Head of Revenue / Growth / Practice Excellence / Sales Enablement, or COO where commercial ops live, or Managing Partner where no named role exists." Added the employee-count translation (200-2,000 headcount matches the 50-500 rep band once you count billable seniors). Added signal-based qualifiers (PE pressure, practice expansion, RevOps job posts, new growth hire) because a pure demographic ICP would have produced a useless list of 600 firms.
- **Why the change matters:** Without the signal layer, the list would have been "every mid-market consultancy in North America" and the prioritization would have been coin-flips. With the signal layer, every account has a defensible reason to be on the list and a defensible reason to be in its specific slot.

### Interaction 2. First cold email draft

- **Task:** Write the Touch 1 cold email for the top account.
- **AI output:** First draft opened with "Hope this finds you well" and had a value prop that said Meridian was "a revenue intelligence platform that uses AI to deliver industry-leading forecast accuracy." Generic SaaS phrasing, no specifics, could have been any vendor.
- **What Ariel changed and why:** Deleted the opener entirely. Replaced the value prop with "Meridian sits on top of Salesforce pipeline data and flags the specific pursuits where partner-reported confidence and behavioral signal have drifted apart." Added the line "We do not replace partner judgment. We show you the pursuits where judgment is about to miss," because consulting-firm buyers need to hear that sentence explicitly before they will read further. Added the off-ramp ("happy to send a one-pager") because consulting-firm Heads of Sales Enablement are calendar-hostile and a PDF is a softer yes than a meeting.
- **Why the change matters:** The first version was indistinguishable from every other SaaS cold email in the Head of Sales Enablement's inbox that morning. The revision names a specific moment (practice expansion), a specific pain (partner confidence versus behavioral signal drift), and a specific non-claim (we do not replace partner judgment). Three specifics in an email buy about ten seconds of attention, which is roughly what is needed for a reply.

### Interaction 4. Pipeline & CRM discipline pass (post-grader feedback)

- **Task:** Grader flagged that Pipeline Forecasting & CRM Discipline was effectively absent from the submission. Specifically called out: no stage definitions applied to the 8 accounts, no activity log schema, no forecast with confidence, no deal-risk register. Add the missing operating layer so a teammate knows not just what to say but where each account sits and how to log it.
- **AI output:** Claude's first pass drafted a seven-stage pipeline lifted from a generic SaaS playbook (lead, MQL, SQL, opportunity, proposal, commit, closed-won). That vocabulary does not match how a BDR owning a cold consulting territory actually operates. "MQL" and "SQL" are marketing-ops terms and a greenfield BDR with zero warm leads has no marketing funnel to map to.
- **What Ariel changed and why:** Replaced the generic labels with S0 Target through S5 AE-Owned, named so a BDR can say them out loud in a pipeline review without sounding like they read a textbook. Added the closed-lost variants (no-response, disqualified, not-now) because every cold territory generates those three outcomes and a pipeline without them overstates coverage. Forced the forecast numbers to reflect actual BDR funnel math on 8 cold accounts (1 meeting booked, 4 engaged-no-reply, 3 sent-no-engagement) rather than an optimistic "4-5 meetings from 8 accounts" first draft. Added the signal-decay risk, which Claude had missed on the first pass, because the whole qualification stack depends on signals that go stale inside 90 days.
- **Why the change matters:** Grader was right that a teammate could read the original submission and know what to say but not where to log the account or how to report forecast up the chain. The revised section fixes both. The handoff trigger is the specific bit that keeps the cycle from stalling: premature AE handoffs are the single largest cause of blown 60-90 day cycles, and the three gates (named problem, named champion, implied budget) are the ones an AE will actually enforce at the pre-meeting review.
