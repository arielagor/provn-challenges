# Challenge 89 Deal Assessment: Vantara / Meridian Industrial

## Section A: Deal Strategy & Close Plan

**Stakeholder map.** Marcus Webb is the economic buyer. The 12 percent discretionary-spend pressure runs through his office. Dana Reyes is the champion and the proof. She has pushed for 18 months and produced the 15-hours-per-week pilot number that is the entire ROI case. Priya Nair is the blocker. She has not said no, but her InfoSec sign-off is a hard gate deal desk will not waive. The sequencing problem: the champion has stalled the handoff to the economic buyer for two weeks, and the blocker has no active workstream. Both need to move this week or fiscal close slips.

**Close plan across 27 business days.**

Week 1 (days 1 to 5): Three-way call set by Dana between Dana, Marcus, and me. Separately, Priya booked for the one-day accelerated InfoSec review. Competitive call to Meridian's CPO before Coupa's "strategic partnership" meeting lands.

Week 3 (days 11 to 15): InfoSec sign-off confirmed in writing. Commercial terms locked. Security addendum finalized. Marcus's budget meeting slotted for week 4.

Week 4 (days 16 to 20): Marcus formal approval. Deal desk green light. Legal redlines resolved.

Week 5 (days 21 to 27): Signatures routed. Ten business days before fiscal close hit with margin.

**Named risks and mitigations.**

Risk one: Dana's "don't call Marcus directly" stance has blocked champion-to-CFO handoff for two weeks. One more week of drift and 27 days becomes 22 and the legal cutoff is unmakeable. Mitigation: ask Dana on the next call to set up a three-way with Marcus this week. Not around her, through her. If she cannot set it up by end of day Friday, I ask if there is a finance partner below Marcus I can engage first.

Risk two: Priya's InfoSec concerns are unscheduled work, which means they stay unscheduled. Even a yes from Marcus breaks on deal desk without her sign-off. **Qualification.** Closable in 27 days if the three-way call lands inside week 1 and Priya accepts the accelerated review inside week 1. Those are the two binary gates. If either slips past day 5, the deal ships to next fiscal year. The 15-hours-per-week pilot data plus Dana's 18-month internal push say the deal is real. The Coupa shadow and the budget pressure say it is not a layup.

**Post-close expansion plan (12 months).**

Days 0 to 30 (wedge): Dana's 8-person team. Day 30 criterion: team has offloaded 40 percent of manual spend-analysis time, via time-tracking or self-report. Day 30 QBR with Dana, Marcus, Priya reviews four KPIs: hours saved, supplier-risk flags triaged, sourcing-cycle reduction, NPS across the 8 users. Those four become the Day 90 expansion scorecard.

Days 31 to 90 (second persona): Expand into Finance. Target: Marcus's FP&A lead, via Dana intro. Vantara's spend data feeds FP&A's monthly variance analysis, so the persona sees pull-value, not vendor push. Milestone: 1 FP&A user activated by Day 90, 3 by Day 120.

Days 91 to 180 (geographic): Meridian's 12,000 employees split North America and Europe. Day 90 QBR identifies the EU counterpart. EU data residency echoes Priya's concern; the accelerated-review precedent carries. Milestone: EU pilot by Day 150, +$120K ACV.

Days 181 to 365 (modules and lock): Add Contract Management. Add supplier-risk scoring at supplier-segment level. Extend direct spend into T&E. At renewal (Month 36), target 2-year extension at +40 percent ACV ($476K). Marcus's FY+2 budget cycle locks over summer, so expansion close gets teed up by Month 30.

**Risks.** Dana leaves: by Day 90, two additional active champions (FP&A lead plus one of Dana's reports). Coupa re-attacks at renewal: usage-depth moat by Month 18. Multi-module footprint makes switching punitively expensive. Track "modules per account." Consolidation fatigue: Marcus bought simplification. Every expansion ties to a named operational KPI.

**Metrics (QBR every 90 days).** `expansion_ARR_growth_pct`, `modules_adopted_per_account`, `named_users_per_account`, `time_to_second_persona_activation_days`. Tied to renewal forecast deck, usage dashboard, seat census, activation log. Outcome, not output.

## Section B: Competitive & Objection Strategy

**Coupa.** The dynamic I am trying to control is frame, not feature set. If Coupa's CPO meeting lands first and gets framed as a "strategic partnership," we become the point-solution challenger in every conversation after, regardless of pilot data. Coupa wins on brand familiarity. I do not beat them by listing features. I beat them by getting there first with a narrower, sharper claim.

Next five business days: Ask Dana for a warm intro to the CPO this week, positioned as a 20-minute pilot readout, not a sales meeting. The pitch is specific: "Dana's team has measurable hours saved. I want you to hear it from her before you hear about anything else." That gets us in the CPO's head with the only number that matters before Coupa shows up with a deck. Secondary move: ask Dana to share the pilot data inside her own network at Meridian so the story is told by Meridian people, not by Vantara.

If the CPO meeting cannot be landed first, fallback is a joint email from Dana to the CPO with pilot metrics attached, sent before Coupa's meeting. Data in the room first, even if I am not.

**Budget objection from Marcus.** When Marcus proposes a 1-year contract or seat reduction, the wrong move is discounting. Discounting teaches him that the original price was soft and invites a second cut. The reframe: a 1-year term is more expensive than a 3-year term for his risk profile, not less.

The language: "Marcus, a 1-year contract is a churn bet. If the pilot numbers hold, in twelve months you are renegotiating this in front of a tighter board than you have today. If the numbers don't hold, you can exit at renewal either way. The 3-year term locks your software inflation at the rate we sign today, which is the only hedge you have against a fiscal-26 spend cap tightening further. It also includes multi-year uplift protection that a 1-year deal cannot offer. Your team has already saved 15 hours per week per analyst. Taking seats out of that equation removes the capacity you need to hit the 12 percent target across the rest of the business."

If he pushes for a seat reduction: review seat utilization at the 12-month mark with a documented true-up path, not a cut at signing. Contract holds. Optionality created. No price concession.
